Kénitra's Geography of Value

Centre Ville commands a 40–60% premium over peripheral zones. Understanding which part of the city you are buying in matters as much as the asking price per square metre.

Published
May 16, 2026
By
Rahim International
Read time
7 minutes

Kénitra is not one market. It is eight distinct zones separated by geography as much as by price — and understanding that geography is the first thing any serious buyer needs to do. The Oued Sebou bisects the city from east to west. The railway and road axes create hard corridors. The Atlantic coast pulls a separate orbit of seasonal demand. Before you read a single price per square metre, you need to know which side of these divides your target asset sits on.

The practical consequence is a price range that runs from 5,500 MAD per square metre for older second-hand stock in the peripheral zones to 15,000 MAD per square metre for premium haut-standing new builds in Centre Ville. That is not noise. That is structure — and it reflects durable differences in access, services, and demand profile that are unlikely to compress quickly.

Centre Ville: The Premium Core

Centre Ville — anchored by Maamora, Mimosas, La Ville Haute, Val Fleuri, El Mellah, and the Campus Universitaire — is the city's historic, civic, and commercial heart, and its price anchor. New apartments here average 10,000–12,500 MAD per square metre with premium haut-standing units reaching 15,000. Second-hand stock trades at 7,000–10,500 depending on sub-neighbourhood and building quality.

The premium is not purely aspirational. The LGV station sits inside Centre Ville, walkable from every sub-neighbourhood — 5 to 12 minutes on foot. That means Rabat is 14 minutes away and Casablanca is 55. The zone concentrates administrative employment, university population, retail, and banking. Rental demand is structural rather than cyclical. Centre Ville consistently produces the city's strongest resale liquidity, which matters as much as the entry yield for buyers thinking about a five-to-ten-year hold.

"The city's geography is sharper than most buyers expect. Two apartments at the same headline price can represent very different investment decisions depending on which zone they sit in."

Ali Rahim · Founder, Rahim International

The Growth Corridors

South Kénitra — primarily Bir Rami Ouest, Bir Rami Est, and Jennan — is the city's most active mid-market growth band, sitting 15 to 22 minutes from the LGV station. New stock here runs 6,500–9,000 MAD per square metre, with a high concentration of Daam Sakani Tier 2 eligible units in the 300,001–700,000 MAD range. For buyers who need commuter access to Rabat but cannot absorb Centre Ville pricing, South Kénitra is the natural alternative.

Kasbah Mehdia — encompassing Addoha, Alliance, and the ancient kasbah hill — sits at the next tier. New stock at Alliance trades at 7,400–9,000 MAD per square metre; the Addoha used-apartment market sits at 200,000–250,000 MAD in total price terms. This is the zone where Daam Sakani Tier 1 inventory (at or below 300,000 MAD, subsidy 100,000 MAD) is most abundantly available — structurally important for first-time buyers and MRE buyers using the programme's bail gratuit arrangement. The zone has changed dramatically over two decades: new roads connect it to the Route Nationale, schools and businesses have followed the housing, and what was once a remote peripheral corridor now functions as an established residential area.

Daam Sakani by zone: Tier 1 (≤300,000 MAD, subsidy 100,000 MAD) is most available in Kasbah Mehdia Region, West Kénitra, South-East Kénitra, and North Kénitra. Tier 2 (300,001–700,000 MAD, subsidy 70,000 MAD) is most available in South Kénitra, East Kénitra (Al Montazah), and West Kénitra (El Haouzia). Centre Ville Tier 2 exists but is limited to smaller units — Centre Ville prices constrain eligibility.

The Coastal Exception and the Entry Belt

Mehdia Beach operates on entirely different logic from the urban zones. Buy prices start from 12,000 MAD per square metre for new coastal stock — a figure that places it above much of the urban market despite sitting 25 to 35 minutes from the LGV station. The driver is seasonal: a well-positioned 2BR can achieve peak summer rental returns that are multiples of its annual long-let value. For second-home buyers, short-term rental investors, and MRE buyers wanting an Atlantic retreat, Mehdia's logic is sound on its own terms. It is simply a different product from the commuter-and-resident market that drives the urban zones.

West Kénitra, East Kénitra, South-East Kénitra, and North Kénitra represent the city's most accessible price points. El Haouzia (Haddada) in West Kénitra — one of the better-served of the peripheral sub-neighbourhoods, with recent private school and café development — trades at 6,500–9,000 MAD per square metre new and 5,500–7,500 second-hand. At the outer edge of the south-east belt, Saknia's newer stock trades at 7,500–9,000 MAD per square metre, reflecting a fully matured urban district that was the city's edge thirty years ago. The older hay chaabi neighbourhoods of East Kénitra — Hay Salam, Hay Errabii, Hay Nahda, Hay Al Amal — carry rent ranges of 1,800–2,500 MAD per month, representing the floor of the long-let market.

These entry zones are not structurally weak. They serve genuine housing demand from industrial workers, civil servants, and local families with deep roots in each district. But they carry lower resale liquidity than the central and growth zones — a trade-off that suits long-term owner-occupiers and Daam Sakani buyers more than investors seeking flexible exit options within a short horizon.

The discipline Kénitra requires is the discipline any geographically segmented market requires: knowing which zone you are actually in, why that zone carries the price it does, and whether the underlying logic matches your use case. The headline price per square metre is the beginning of the analysis, not the end of it.
