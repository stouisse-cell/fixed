Kénitra — Complete Neighbourhood Guide

Twelve Zones.
One City to Understand.

Kénitra's property market spans an enormous range — from roughly 5,500 MAD/m² for older middle-belt stock to 15,000 MAD/m² for premium central builds. A city-wide average tells you almost nothing. This guide reads the city zone by zone: buy prices, long-let rents, Daam Sakani fit, and LGV access time for all 40 sub-neighbourhoods.

At a glance

12 residential zones, 40 sub-neighbourhoods, one complete map.

Kénitra is not a large city, but its neighbourhoods are not as connected as a map implies. The Sebou river, the Royal Air Force base, a military complex close to the urban core, the Maamora forest, and limited arterial roads mean two areas that appear close on screen can be a 30-minute drive apart at peak hours. Understanding those divisions is as important as understanding price per square metre.

City Overview

The Market Reads
Best from the Centre Out.

Kénitra sits 40 km north of Rabat on the Atlantic coast. The LGV connects it to Rabat in roughly 14 minutes and Casablanca in roughly 55 minutes, but the city does not price as one continuous market. Centre Ville behaves as the premium core, the university and east-central fabric trade on convenience and density, Mehdia follows coastal logic, and the western and southern belts each have their own affordability ladder.

The Sebou river defines the northern edge. The Royal Air Force base occupies a large portion of the northwest. A military complex sits close to the urban core. Train tracks divide the historic fabric from the rest of the city. And with a limited number of arterial roads connecting one side to the other, a neighbourhood that appears close on screen can mean a 30-minute drive at peak hours. In Kénitra, geography is not just background — it is part of the purchase decision.

01

Zone 01 — True Centre

Centre Ville

Quartier Maamora / Le villageQuartier des MimosasLa Ville HauteVal Fleury

This is the historic, civic, and commercial core of Kénitra, and for many buyers it remains the most practical part of the city. It offers the strongest walkability, the densest concentration of daily services, and the clearest link to the LGV station and the rest of the Rabat-Casablanca corridor. This is the side of Kénitra where daily life works on foot: shops, cafés, schools, clinics, banks, taxis, and civic spaces all sit within the same urban fabric.

Maamora and Mimosas represent the strongest part of the central premium, combining older stock with newer, more polished residences and some of the city's strongest rental appeal. La Ville Haute stretches toward the Corniche Sebou side, offering a broader mix of apartments and villa pockets at a wider price band. Val Fleury sits at the core's edge — primarily mature villas on established plots from the 1970s and 1980s — and occupies a distinct position as the central zone's villa market.

Zone 02 — East Of The Core

East-Central Kénitra

El MellahQuartier GenieLa CigogneLa ChenilleJarda al Qadi

East-Central Kénitra is the first residential ring east of the core, separated from it by Avenue Moulay Youssef and the old rail corridor. El Mellah anchors the zone with a distinct identity: older buildings, a cluster of jewellers, the Gare Routière — the city's main bus station — and a built fabric that is being selectively demolished and rebuilt, producing a mixed profile of historic stock alongside gradual redevelopment.

Quartier Genie, La Cigogne, La Chenille, and Jarda al Qadi extend the zone eastward into practical everyday residential territory — denser, more mixed, and consistently more affordable than the premium core, while still close enough to the centre to draw on its services and infrastructure. For buyers who want central adjacency without paying full Centre Ville pricing, this zone is the natural first comparison.

Zone 03 — University Belt

Campus Universitaire

Campus Universitaire Ibn Tofail

Campus Universitaire sits south of the rail corridor, separated from the central fabric by the train tracks — a boundary that shapes how this zone functions as a market. Its primary driver is sustained rental demand from Ibn Tofail University: students, university staff, and associated service workers make up a large share of the tenant pool, creating a steady occupancy pattern tied to the academic calendar rather than commuter cycles.

New supply here is limited and the resale market consists largely of smaller apartments bought specifically to let. This is a usage-led zone rather than a prestige one — less image-driven than Centre Ville, but with a fairly predictable income logic for rental investors who understand the product and the tenant base they are buying into.

Zone 04 — North-West Growth Corridor

Kasbah Mehdia

Kasbah El MehdiaAlliance MehdiaAddoha (Residence du Golf)Zone Villa du Golf

This corridor has undergone one of Kénitra’s most visible neighbourhood transformations over the past two decades. Addoha began as government-subsidised low-income housing blocks and has since grown into a busy, lived-in community with schools, shops, and everyday services fully integrated — with Marjane anchoring nearby commercial activity and continued upward pressure on prices. Alliance started from a similar base but has evolved further still: new mid-range apartment developments, villa zones, a mall, and expanding commercial strips now give it the character of one of Kénitra’s more active growth districts.

Kasbah El Mehdia sits on the hill overlooking the beach, on the site of the ancient kasbah. Once associated with informal housing, it has been progressively transformed since the 1990s — particularly after Al Omrane released plots for construction — into a mix of R+2 and R+3 houses, villa areas, and older urban fabric with a very different profile from the flat beachfront strip below. Zone Villa du Golf completes the picture with a quieter, lower-density villa option on the zone’s eastern edge.

Zone 05 — Atlantic Strip

Mehdia Beach

Plage MehdiaPort de Mehdia

Mehdia Beach is an entirely different market from the city behind it. This is Kénitra's Atlantic-facing strip: beachfront apartments, seaside villas, seafront cafés, and leisure-oriented residential stock concentrated along one of Morocco's more accessible Atlantic beaches. The logic here is coastal lifestyle — summer occupancy, second-home ownership, and short-let positioning — not daily commuting or urban convenience.

Port de Mehdia adds a working fishing harbour character alongside the seasonal beach economy, with a more year-round residential population than the pure beachfront strip. Pricing starts from 12,000 MAD/m² for apartments in meaningful coastal positions and climbs for prime sea-view stock or newer builds. Long-let rents run well above the inland city average, and summer short-let rates behave very differently again — making this a zone that requires its own investment logic, not a straightforward comparison with inland alternatives.

Zone 06 — Immediate Southern Ring

South-Central Kénitra

Bir Rami OuestBir Rami EstMaghreb Al Arabi

South-Central Kénitra opens with Bir Rami Ouest, widely regarded as one of the city's most prestigious residential addresses. Known for its larger plots, mature streets, and established villa character, it is the closest thing Kénitra has to a luxury residential neighbourhood — primarily a villa market with a settled feel built over decades, not a new-build zone.

Bir Rami Est is more mixed in profile: newer residential development alongside older stock, with proximity to the ZI Bir Rami industrial zone shaping its southern boundary. Maghreb Al Arabi extends the practical apartment-led fabric of the immediate southern ring. Together they form the first coherent residential belt below the centre — not the outer periphery, but genuine family residential territory with strong everyday ties to the core.

Zone 07 — Deeper Southern Belt

South Kénitra

Bir Rami SudAl Hadika 1Al Hadika 2Jenane

South Kénitra is the fuller southward expansion belt that continues the city's residential logic toward Rabat. Jenane has developed into an active zone of R+3 and R+4 apartment buildings alongside newer private developments, following the steady outward-growth trajectory that has been reshaping this side of the city for over a decade. Al Hadika 1 and 2, and Bir Rami Sud, complete the zone with a predominantly owner-occupier residential fabric.

These are not prestige addresses, but they are coherent, built-up neighbourhoods with growing commercial infrastructure and increasingly clear pricing. For buyers who need to maximise their budget without moving into genuinely peripheral territory, this belt consistently offers more for the money than the inner zones — and the sustained southward growth trend reinforces the long-term logic of buying here.

Zone 08 — Central-West Ring

West-Central Kénitra

TaibiaLa CiteLe VallonQuartier Sayad

West-Central Kénitra runs along the Sebou side of the city, combining the quieter riverfront character of La Cité and Taïbia with the broader residential practicality of Le Vallon and Quartier Sayad. La Cité occupies a distinctive riverfront position north of La Ville Haute — calmer and more residential in feel than the commercial centre, yet close enough to draw on it without difficulty. Taïbia shares the same Sebou-side quality: more settled than speculative, with a lived-in residential profile built up over time rather than a new-build promotional one.

Le Vallon and Quartier Sayad fill the zone with mid-market apartment stock that sits noticeably closer to the central fabric than the deeper western belt — a meaningful difference when estimating both commute times and access to central amenities. For buyers who want the value of a west-of-centre address without moving as far out as Oulad Oujih or Al Haouzia, this is the zone to examine first.

Zone 09 — Deeper Western Belt

West Kénitra

Oulad OujihAl HaouziaIsmailiaAl Haddadda

West Kénitra spans a wider range than its collective label might suggest. Oulad Oujih has developed over decades into a large mixed zone of houses, villas, and apartments with its own commercial activity and everyday services — a proper neighbourhood in the city's fabric rather than a peripheral housing zone. Al Haouzia (also known as Haddada) has undergone a genuine residential upgrade in recent years: new apartment blocks, green spaces, private schools, cafés, restaurants, and boutique food shops have transformed it into one of the city's more attractive new middle-class residential areas, with a villa zone alongside the Route de Mehdia adding a lower-density option.

Ismailia and Al Haddadda belong to the same practical middle-belt logic: established, lived-in stock with everyday services and a building profile ranging from older houses to newer apartments. For buyers who need value without sacrificing a functional city address, the western belt consistently delivers more per square metre than the centre — and Al Haouzia in particular has been changing faster than its address implies.

Zone 10 — Eastern Residential City

East Kénitra

Ancienne Medina (Khabazzate)Quartiers Populaires de la MedinaAl MontazahBab Fes

East Kénitra tells the clearest story of how the city actually lives, beyond the polished promotional image. Al Montazah stands apart from the rest of the zone: a relatively recent residential neighbourhood that mixes new apartment buildings and villas with green spaces, a public garden, schools, restaurants, and cafés — noticeably greener and more open in character than much of the city's newer stock, and consistently the zone's strongest market segment.

The Ancienne Medina and Khabazzate form one continuous traditional area defined by narrow alleys, older buildings, dense street life, and the kinds of informal and semi-formal commercial activity that give older Moroccan city centres their energy. Open-air markets, a cluster of jewellers, food vendors, artisanal workshops, and a public-life rhythm that can feel — on a smaller scale — reminiscent of Jemaa el-Fnaa make this the city's most characterful quarter. Hay Salam, Hay Errabii, Hay Nahda, and Hay Al Amal extend the same fabric: deeply rooted hay chaabi neighbourhoods with local ovens (ferran), street vendors, and a coexistence of traditional street life with gradual modernisation that defines this side of Kénitra. Bab Fes bridges the eastern and central fabric with a more varied profile that includes newer elements alongside the historic east.

Zone 11 — South-East Growth Edge

South-East Kénitra

SakniaHay El OufaaAin Sebaa

Saknia tells a story of steady urban maturation. Thirty years ago it marked where Kénitra effectively ended. Today it is a fully integrated residential district with planned streets, commercial activity, banks, restaurants, and everyday services woven into the neighbourhood fabric. The trajectory has been consistent: infrastructure follows population, and the area now functions as a proper district rather than an outer-city extension.

Hay El Oufaa and Ain Sebaa sit further out on the same southeastward arc, following the road toward Rabat. These are newer and less complete than Saknia, with a stronger new-build character and a more emerging commercial infrastructure. The Forêt Urbaine and the city-entry road frame this side of Kénitra geographically and give the zone a legible identity — though the forest and the entry road are spatial anchors, not housing sub-markets in themselves.

Zone 12 — Northern Edge

North Kénitra

Quartier l'AssamFouarate

North Kénitra rarely appears in polished real-estate marketing, but Quartier l'Assam and Fouarate remain part of the city's everyday residential fabric. These are local neighbourhoods in the truest sense: more visible to the people who live in them than to buyers approaching the city through listings or developer brochures. Pricing visibility on mainstream portals is thin, which means the ground-level picture diverges more significantly from online data here than almost anywhere else in Kénitra.

For buyers approaching this area, direct local market contact matters more than benchmark comparison. The appeal, where it exists, is entirely about entry-level cost — not image, infrastructure quality, or commute convenience. If those trade-offs work for your situation, you will find less competition and more room to negotiate here than in any other part of the city.
